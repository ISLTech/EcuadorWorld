History

An orrery is a mechanical model of the Solar System that demonstrates or predicts the relative positions and motions of the planets and moons, typically based on the heliocentric model. It can also depict the relative sizes of these celestial bodies; however, due to the vast differences in actual size ratios, a simplified approximation is often used instead. The Greeks had functioning planetaria, but the first modern-era planetarium was produced around 1712 by John Rowley. He named it "orrery" in honor of Charles Boyle, 4th Earl of Orrery (in County Cork, Ireland). The plaque on the device reads: "Orrery invented by Graham 1700, improved by Rowley, and presented by him to the Earl of Orrery, after whom it was named at the suggestion of Richard Steele."


CERES

Ceres, the minor body in Solar System No. 1, is a dwarf planet and the largest astronomical object in the asteroid belt, the region of the Solar System that lies between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter. It was the first asteroid discovered, on January 1, 1801, by the astronomer and priest Giuseppe Piazzi from the Astronomical Observatory of Palermo in Italy. Originally considered a planet, it was reclassified as an asteroid in the 1850s when other objects in similar orbits began to be discovered. At the beginning of the 21st century, after the redefinition of "planet", it was reclassified as a dwarf planet, and is also the only one completely within the orbit of Neptune. Its diameter of approximately 945 km makes it the 33rd largest known object in the Solar System. Its mass is estimated to be one third of the total mass of the asteroid belt, making it the only object in the belt to have reached hydrostatic equilibrium.

ASTEROID BELT

The asteroid belt is a torus-shaped region in the Solar System, centered on the Sun and spanning roughly the space between the orbits of the planets Jupiter and Mars. It contains a large number of irregularly shaped solid bodies called asteroids or minor planets. The identified objects are of many sizes, but much smaller than planets and are on average about a million kilometers (or six hundred thousand miles) apart. This asteroid belt is also called the main asteroid belt or main belt to distinguish it from other asteroid populations in the Solar System.

COMETS

Comets are frozen remnants from the formation of the solar system made of dust, rock and ice. They range in size from a few kilometres to tens of kilometres across, but as they orbit closer to the Sun, they heat up and spew out gases and dust into a bright head that can be larger than a planet. This material forms a tail that stretches for millions of kilometres.

MAKEMAKE

Makemake is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, the third largest planet in the Solar System and one of the largest objects outside our solar neighborhood. It has one known satellite, nicknamed MK2. Its diameter is about 60% that of Pluto.

KUIPER BELT

The Kuiper Belt is a circumstellar disk in the outer Solar System, extending from the orbit of Neptune at 30 astronomical units (AU) to about 50 AU from the Sun. It is similar to the asteroid belt, but is much larger—20 times wider and between 20 and 200 times more massive. Like the asteroid belt, it is composed mostly of small bodies or remnants from when the Solar System formed. While many asteroids are composed primarily of rock and metal, most Kuiper Belt objects are composed largely of frozen volatiles (referred to as "ices"), such as methane, ammonia, and water. The Kuiper Belt is home to most of the objects that astronomers generally accept as dwarf planets: Orcus, Pluto, Haumea, Quaoar, and Makemake. Some of the Solar System's moons, such as Neptune's Triton and Saturn's Phoebe, may have originated in the region.

ERIS

Eris is a dwarf planet in the Solar System. It is the most massive trans-Neptunian object, the second largest after Pluto, and the largest body in the Solar System not to have been visited by a space probe. It has a diameter of 2,326 ± 12 km, slightly smaller than Pluto's 2,377 ± 2 km, and a mass 27% greater than that of Pluto. In contrast, it represents only 0.27% of the mass of the Earth. It was discovered in January 2005 by a team at the Palomar Observatory led by Michael E. Brown; its identity was verified later that year. It is a trans-Neptunian object (TNO), a member of a population of high-eccentricity objects known as the scattered disk, and has a satellite named Dysnomia. These objects have been pulled into a more distant orbit than usual by gravitational interactions with Neptune in the early stages of the formation of the Solar System. As of February 2016, its distance from the Sun was 96.3 AU, about three times that of Pluto. With the exception of a few comets, Eris and Dysnomia were then the second-farthest natural objects in the Solar System (the farthest being V774104 discovered in November 2015 at 103 AU).


SUN

The Sun is the star at the centre of the Solar System. It is a massive, almost perfect sphere of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions at its core, which radiates energy from its surface mainly in the form of visible light and infrared radiation, with 10% in ultraviolet energies. It is by far the most important source of energy for life on Earth. The Sun has been an object of veneration in many cultures. It has been a central subject of astronomical research since ancient times.

MERCURY

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the nearest to the Sun.Mercury is only slightly larger than Earth's Moon. Its surface is covered in tens of thousands of impact craters. Despite its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is not the hottest planet in our solar system – that title belongs to nearby Venus, thanks to its dense atmosphere. But Mercury is the fastest planet, zipping around the Sun every 88 Earth days. Mercury is appropriately named for the swiftest of the ancient Roman gods.

VENUS

Venus is the second planet in the Solar System in order of proximity to the Sun and the third smallest after Mercury and Mars. Like Mercury, it has no natural satellites. It is named after Venus, the Roman goddess of love (in Ancient Greece, Aphrodite). As the second brightest natural object after the Moon, it can be seen in a clear night sky with the naked eye. It appears at dawn and at dusk. Because of the distances of the orbits of Venus and Earth from the Sun, Venus is never visible more than three hours before sunrise or three hours after sunset.

EARTH

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known astronomical object to support life. This is possible because Earth is an ocean world, the only one in the Solar System to support liquid water on the surface. Nearly all of Earth's water is contained in its global ocean, which covers 70.8% of the Earth's crust. The remaining 29.2% of the Earth's crust is land, most of which is in the form of continental landmasses within Earth's terrestrial hemisphere. Most of Earth's land is somewhat moist and covered with vegetation, while large ice sheets in Earth's polar deserts hold more water than Earth's groundwater, lakes, rivers, and atmospheric water combined. Earth's crust is made up of slowly moving tectonic plates, which interact to produce mountain ranges, volcanoes, and earthquakes. The Earth has a liquid outer core that creates a magnetosphere capable of deflecting most of the destructive solar winds and cosmic radiation.

MOON

The Moon's far side gets as much sunlight as its near side.Like Earth, the Moon has a day side and a night side, which change as the Moon rotates. The Sun always illuminates half of the Moon while the other half remains dark.NASA’s LRO mission has used its seven science instruments to map the entire lunar surface, including the Moon's near and far sides, down to a scale of one meter.

MARS

Mars – the fourth planet from the Sun – is a dusty, cold, desert world with a very thin atmosphere. This dynamic planet has seasons, polar ice caps, extinct volcanoes, canyons and weather.

JUPITER

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system. If Jupiter was a hollow shell, 1,000 Earths could fit inside. Jupiter also is the oldest planet, forming from the dust and gases left over from the Sun's formation 4.5 billion years ago. But it has the shortest day in the solar system, taking only 10.5 hours to spin around once on its axis.

SATURN

Saturn is a massive ball made mostly of hydrogen and helium. The farthest planet from Earth discovered by the unaided human eye, Saturn has been known since ancient times. The planet is named for the Roman god of agriculture and wealth, who was also the father of Jupiter.

URANUS

Uranus is a very cold and windy planet. It is surrounded by faint rings, and more than two dozen small moons as it rotates at a nearly 90-degree angle from the plane of its orbit. This unique tilt makes Uranus appear to spin on its side. Uranus is blue-green in color due to large amounts of methane, which absorbs red light but allows blues to be reflected back into space.

NEPTUNE

More than 30 times as far from the Sun as Earth, Neptune is not visible to the naked eye. The planet’s blue color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red wavelengths of light, but allows blue ones to be reflected back into space – very much like its neighbor, Uranus. Neptune was the first planet located using math. German astronomer Johann Galle was the first to observe the planet in 1846. The planet is named after the Roman god of the sea.

PLUTO

Pluto was long considered our ninth planet, but the International Astronomical Union reclassified Pluto as a dwarf planet in 2006. NASA's New Horizons was the first spacecraft to explore Pluto up close, flying by in 2015. Pluto was discovered in 1930 by astronomer Clyde Tombaugh. It was named by 11-year-old Venetia Burney of Oxford, England.
